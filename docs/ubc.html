Opening 1N

1N is the best defined opening bid that there is. It shows 15-17 pts1 and a balanced hand.

Reminder: A balanced hand is one with no singleton or void (suits with one or zero cards in them respectively) and at most one doubleton.

Possible balanced shapes:

  • 43332
  • 4432
  • 5332

Examples of non-balanced shapes:

  • 5330 (contains a void)
  • 4441 (contains a singleton)
  • 5422 (contains more than one doubleton)

Responding to 1N: a brief overview

  • 1N — 15-17 pts, balanced
    • 2♣ — Stayman: 8+ pts, 4+♥ or 4+♠
    • 2♦ — Transfer to ♥: 5+♥
    • 2♠ — Transfer to ♠: 5+♠
    • 2N — 8-9 pts, invitational to 3N
    • 3N — 10+ pts, to play

Quick way to find what you need:

  • If you have a 4 card major, see Stayman,
  • If you have a 5+ card major, see Transfers,
  • If you have no major longer than 4 cards, see 2N/3N.

2♣ — Stayman

Stayman is an asking bid, used to find out whether a partnership has an 8-card major suit fit. The bidder of Stayman must have at least one four card major, and he/she is asking the 1N opener if they have any four card majors so that they may find a fit.

Stayman shows 8 or more points, so if you have 0-7 you must pass 1N.

The opener's responses are:

  • 1N–2♣ — Stayman
    • 2♦ — no 4-card major (3-♥, 3-♠)
    • 2♥ — four cards in ♥3
    • 2♠ — four cards in ♠, 3-♥

After hearing partner's response to Stayman, the responder continues with the auction based on whether they and their partner have found a fit.

If they have not found a fit, responder continues by bidding N at a level which is appropriate for the strength of his hand. (Remember you need need 25 pts between you and your partner to go for game. Partner has shown 15-17, so with 8-9 pts you want to invite him to bid game and with 10 or more you want to bid game yourself):

  • 1N–2♣–2♦ — no 4-card major
    • 2N — 8-9 pts, invitational
      • pass — 15-16 pts
      • 3N — 17 pts
    • 3N — 10+ pts, to play

These responses have the same meaning after, for example, 1N–2♣–2♠: opener doesn't have four cards in ♠, so there is no major-suit fit and we are trying to find the correct No Trumps contract to play in.

If they have found a fit, responder continues by raising partner's major suit to a level which is appropriate for the strength of his hand:

  • 1N–2♣–2♥ — four cards in ♥
    • 3♥ — 4+♥, 8-9 pts, invitational
      • pass — 15-16 pts
      • 4♥ — 17 pts
    • 4♥ — 4+♥, 10 pts, to play

2♦ and 2♥ — Transfers

Unlike Stayman, Transfers are a showing bid, which means they give information to your partner as opposed to asking him/her for information about their hand. Bidding a transfer shows 5+ cards in the suit above the one you bid i.e.

  • 1N — 15-17 pts, balanced
    • 2♦ — 5 or more ♥
    • 2♥ — 5 or more ♠

Unlike Stayman, Transfers do not promise a minimum point count and can be done with as few as 0 pts.

After the responder has made a transfer bid, the opener is obliged to bid the next suit up (the suit that the responder intended to transfer to). The reason for this is that partner may have as few as 0 pts and therefore you must give him the option to pass it out at the 2-level as his hand would not be strong enough to bid higher.

After the opener has completed the transfer, responder now must decide whether to set his major as trumps or whether to offer opener the choice between No Trumps and playing with the major you have shown as trumps.

As opener has promised us a balanced hand we know that he holds at least 2 cards in our major. Therefore, if we have 6 or more cards in our major, we can be certain that we want to set that suit as trumps4. In this case we will raise partner to 3 of the major (invitational, 8-9pts) or 4 of the major (10+ pts) accordingly.

If we only have five cards in the major, we want to offer partner the choice of playing in our major (which he will choose if he holds 3 card support for our major) or in N (which he will choose if he holds a doubleton). We do this by rebiding N at the appropriate level (2N - invitational, 8-9pts; 3N, 10+pts)

  • 1N–2♦ — transfer to ♥5
    • 2♥ — transfer completed (only available bid)
      • pass — to play (at most 7 pts)
      • 2N — 8-9 pts, exactly five ♥
        • ...
      • 3♥ — 8-9 pts, 6+♥
        • pass — 15-16 pts
        • 4♥ — 17 pts
      • 3N — 10+ pts, exactly five ♥
        • pass — two cards in ♥
        • 4♥ — 3+♥
      • 4♥ — 10+ pts, 6+♥

After 2N, opener chooses both the level of the final contract (whether he will bid game or not) and if it will be in ♥ or in N. With at least three cards in ♥, we have an 8-card fit and should be playing in 3♥ or 4♥. Otherwise, we should be playing 2N or 3N:

  • 1N–2♦–2♥–2N — 8-9 pts, 5♥
    • pass — 15-16 pts, 2♥
    • 3♥ — 15-16 pts, 3+♥
    • 3N — 17 pts, 2♥
    • 4♥ — 17 pts, 3+♥

2N opening

A 2N opening and its responses are almost analogous to the 1N opening. The only difference being that the point ranges have changed.

A 2N opening shows 20-21 pts and a balanced distribution. Therefore if your partner opens 2N you should bid game if you have more than 4 pts, but pass (or transfer to a 5 card major and then pass) if you have 0-3 pts.

The responses are identical to the responses over 1NT and their continuations are the same.

  • 2N — 20-21 pts, balanced
    • 3♣ — Stayman
      • 3♦ — 3-♥, 3-♠
      • 3♥ — 4+♥
        • 3N — 3-♥ (no ♥ fit)6
        • 4♥ — 4+♥
      • 3♠ — 4+♠, 3-♥
        • 3N — 3-♠ (no ♠ fit)
        • 4♠ — 4+♠
    • 3♦ — transfer to ♥: 5+♥
      • 3♥ — transfer completed
        • 3N — five ♥, choice between 3N and 4♥
      • 4♥ — 6+♥, to play
    • 3♥ — transfer to ♠: 5+♠
      • ... — as after 3♦–3♥
    • 3N — 5+ pts, 3-♥, 3-♠, to play

The key difference one should note is that after a 2N opening there is no space to invite partner to game. This is the reason why the 2N opening point range is narrower than the 1N opening point range.
